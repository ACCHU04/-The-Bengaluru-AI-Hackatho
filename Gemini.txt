Your React app is the communication hub. It continuously records your voice in tiny chunks (every 250 milliseconds) and streams them instantly over a WebSocket. It doesn't wait for you to finish your sentence; it streams live.

2. The Ear (Deepgram)
The backend routes those audio chunks directly to Deepgram. Deepgram is your "Ear." It is exceptionally good at ignoring background noise (like ambulance sirens). As you speak, Deepgram fires back text transcripts to your server. It sends "interim" transcripts (guessing what you are saying mid-sentence) and "final" transcripts (the completed thought).

3. The Brain & Hands (Gemini 1.5 Flash)
Once Deepgram gives us a "final" transcript, we hand it to Gemini. Because we used a strict system prompt, Gemini knows it's a medical AI. But Gemini isn't just a brain; we gave it Hands (Tool Calling).

If you say, "What's the protocol for burns?", Gemini just answers back.

But if you say, "Patient has severe burns, HR 120," Gemini realizes it needs to act. It outputs a JSON command to your Node.js server to trigger the log_vitals tool. Your server executes the action (the "Hands"), tells Gemini it was successful, and Gemini prepares to speak.

4. The Mouth (Cartesia)
Gemini's final text response is instantly streamed to Cartesia, which converts the text into a hyper-realistic human voice buffer and pipes it straight back to your phone to play out loud.

5. The Reflex (Barge-in Logic)
This is what wins the hackathon. If Aegis is speaking, but you suddenly shout, "Wait, HR is 140!", Deepgram immediately hears you and generates a new "interim" transcript. Your server spots this and instantly fires a {"type": "clear_buffer"} missile to your React app. Your React app violently kills the ongoing audio. To the user, it feels like the AI instantly stopped talking to listen to your correction.
